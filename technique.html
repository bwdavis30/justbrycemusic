Technique

First, the most important part of drumming is the technique. Without the proper guided technique, you simply cannot put your first notes down correctly. In this section of learning technique, we will dive into how to comfortably hold your sticks, introducing a few basic strokes to help adjust your grip for your sticks. After all, grips come somewhat personally to the drummer considering the slight differences in physique and equipment.

Now, it's important to keep in mind the many types of grips that can be approached in rudimental drumming. However, we will mainly learn and critique the two fundamental grips that serve as the root of proper technique - match grip and traditional grip. You will also be lightly introduced to a few other grips that have culturally derived from the main grips to allow you to tackle a wide variety of learning.

Match Grip

The first grip, and by far the most important grip, is match grip. In all instances, match grip should be the first grip that you learn before any other, as it is the pure foundation to all techniques in stick gripping. So, first, grab yourself a nice pair of sticks. Depending on the type of drum you are looking to focus your technique on, your stick type could vary from thin, light sticks for drumset to heavy, plastic-tip sticks for tenor drums. However, I would begin with a pair of drumsticks that are versitile for all instances. Since we are focusing on rudimental drumming, a pair of rudimental snare sticks would do (sometimes called corpsmasters, marching hybrids, or something of the matter depending on the company).

Next, the technique. Let's focus on holding the stick in your hand. As hinted in the name, match grip is the drumming grip in which your left hand matches your right hand in the positioning of the stick. So, let's begin with the right hand, as it is considered the lead hand:

  1. Give yourself a thumbs up!
  2. Place the stick in your thumbs up, resting the thumb on the stick.
  3. Make sure the butt of your stick is sticking out no more than an inch.
  4. Tuck the thumb down to be symmetric to your index finger.

Congratulations! You're holding a drumstick properly. Don't worry, there's more than just holding it before getting to drumming. To make sure your positioning is proper, you should be able to let go with your bottom three fingers, balancing it with just your index and thumb. If this feels hard to hold, then you are holding the stick wrong. Make sure your nail on your thumb and the middle crease of your index finger are perfectly lined up. It should feel comfortable once you get the proper grip. For the left hand, you're just going to simply copy the same exact thing. If you seem to be getting lost, refer to the video at the bottom of this section.

Finally, let's put that onto the drum. Whether you have a drum, a drum pad, a table, or a pillow, any surface can help you better your technique at this part of learning, but just make sure your surface is to around your waist. This will help later to level out your technique properly when putting sticks onto the drum. To actually put these sticks on the drum with proper technique, you should not simply aim them down to where your thumbs are on top. This is a completely different technique called French grip that should be avoided until match grip is properly learned. Instead, we are going to aim them down, then rotate our hand to where our thumbs are facing inwards. As you turn, the butt of your stick should turn out, and your arm should follow the sticks. Make sure to not hike up your elbows or shoulders. You should feel relaxed - not tensed up. Tensing could cause improper technique when we put notes on the drum. If correct, your arms should be relaxed, thumbs turned inwards, and your sticks should make a triangle with a 90° angle. Then, you should level the backend of both of your sticks to be slightly elevated from the surface, making the impression of the tip being pressed in. When putting on an actual drum, this allows you to avoid unintentionally hitting the rim of the drum.

Well done! You have learned proper match grip! While it may not seem like it, this is already a huge step in learning. It also takes many beginners a long time to perfect this. You may still seem a bit uncomfortable with your grip, or you may think it's totally wrong. That's okay. As you begin to drum, your grip will adjust more comfortably. As long as you refer to the basics of grip and not detour too far from the basics, slight changes are perfectly fine. If you are a beginner, I would strongly suggest skipping over the more advanced grips that are soon to be introduced, and instead skip to "Stick Control." Once you learn all the basics, it will be beneficial to circle back and learn the more advanced techniques later.

Traditional and Advanced Grips

If you already have some experience in drumming, that last part might have bored you a bit. However, it's always important to refer back to the basics. Sometimes, we don't even realize as musicians how often we misinterpret our poor technique to be proper. Always refer to the basics, especially before learning or critiquing more advanced techniques.

The next most used grip is traditional grip. While the use of traditional grip is often debated among percussionists, it is undeniably a core technique in drumming that all drummers should learn. The most common drum in which this grip is used on is snare drum. While it is also sometimes seen on drumset or other concert drums, the main drum that traditional grip is used on is concert and rudimental snare drum. This grip derives from the sling marching snare drum, which were commonly seen in early development of percussion instruments and primarily popularized in marching in the 70's. Traditional grip was necessary for these drums since it placed at an angle, typically on the left hip. This required drummers to hike their left hand up to drum properly. However, while the instrument itself became depopularized, the grip remains prominant in rudimental drumming.

To actually implement traditional grip, we need to first focus on how to hold the sticks. We are going to focus on letting our left hand be the altered grip, considering this is the widely used version of traditional grip. So, our right hand stays the same as match grip, but our left hand will be altered to the following:

  1. Make an okay sign with your hand (create a circle with your index and thumb).
  2. Slide your thumb on the top-most crease of your index finger.
  3. Place the stick in the hole of the okay sign with no more than an inch of the butt.
  4. Rest your fingers on the stick. Index on top, middle straightened out on top, and the ring and pinky supporting under.

Congratulations, you have learned traditional grip! Now let's make sure you're properly holding the stick to the drum. This can be particularily hard with traditional grip, so we will also dive into the proper leveling in regard to the drumming surface as well. As for putting to the drum, it is nearly identical to putting match grip onto the drum. Right hand is exactly the same, but your left hand's thumb should be turned up, not inwards anymore. As for leveling the left hand, it should be slightly elevated as well. Think of your left hand's palm as an imaginary cup. If you were to put water into your palm, it should not firmly hold the water nor spill the water. It should, instead, consistently drip out the water. While that may sound a bit confusing at first, putting your first notes on the drum will help you undertand this much more.

While these are the most common grips, it is important to also realize that there are so many different grips depending on the scenario. For example, another extremely popular grip, commonly used for timpani technique, is french grip. This consists of an altered match grip in which the thumbs stay up instead of rotate inwards. There is also a german grip, american grip, moeller grip, and so much more! I would strongly advise that if you are seeking to learn more about technique, you should dive into these different grips and attempting to critique those, applying them to your specific percussion instruments as well. After all, grips are like a tool. While they some may not be used often, they are great for a specific job.
